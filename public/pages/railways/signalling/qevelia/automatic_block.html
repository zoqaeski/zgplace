Automatic Block Signal Systems

Most colour-light signals are part of an automatic block system, although those in stations are controlled by the signaller and only automatically reset to Stop.

Automatic Block Operation

Automatic block signals operate under a number of differing rules, depending on the level of traffic and operational requirements. In all cases the aspects displayed to the driver remain the same; rather, the rules specify how signal operators and train controllers must direct traffic.

Automatic Block Sequences

There are several different sequences used, differing in the number of clear blocks before an occupied block.

Two-Aspect

Two-aspect block signals are used when block lengths are longer than 4000 m. Distant signals are always provided approximately 1200 metres in rear of the block signal.

The earliest automatic block systems used two-aspect motorised semaphores with motorised distant signals at braking distance in rear. Motorised semaphores remain in use on short sections of just three lines at present.

Three-Aspect

Three-aspect automatic block is fairly common, especially on older lines. As it is relatively easily upgraded to a four-aspect system, most installations have been modified to permit higher speeds. The block length is usually between 1200 and 2400 metres; shorter blocks are rarely used for the three-aspect system.

Four-Aspect

The most common automatic block system, as it permits a mixture of fast and slow trains. Faster and heavier trains start braking at the Advance Caution, other trains brake at the Caution signal. The train control system usually enforces a speed restriction at the Advance Caution; although most trains (passenger trains in particular) could easily come to a halt within a single block length, enforcing a speed restriction two blocks ahead improves line capacity.

An alternative style of multiple-aspect block used Approach Medium instead of Advance Caution, but this was rendered obsolete by the introduction of flashing aspects. In recent years block signals with speed indicators has seen a limited reintroduction of this system, primarily on high-density suburban lines.
