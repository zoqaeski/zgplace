Qevelian Railway Signalling

Main Signals

Main signals protect areas used by train movements, such as stations and the open line. Generally their aspects are not applicable to shunting movements, especially when the shunting signal gives a conflicting indication.

Designs

There are a number of classifications of signals, although unlike some systems the signalling rulebook does not distinguish between controlled and automatic signals. All controlled signals are interlocked with their associated turnout routes, and automatic signals are, by definition, interlocked with their associated block sections.

All semaphore signals are three-position (though not all positions may be operable) and operate in the upper-left quadrant. The earlier designs were worked by the double-wire system in preference to rod-worked or single-wire mechanisms, as it permits operation in three positions over significantly longer distances. Electro-pneumatic and later relay-based interlocking introduced power signalling, operated by compressed air, or more frequently, electric motors. The first automatic block semaphores used motors and relays that imitated the operation of the manual block system.

Colour-light signals were first introduced during electrification of the main lines and later the suburban railway networks of Kirua and Deşva. The aspects are a superset of the night aspects of the semaphore signals, extended by flashing lights. To prevent failure of a bulb resulting in a wrong-side failure, all bulbs have a double filament which is automatically lit if the main one burns out. This filament is slightly out-of-focus so a burnt-out light will appear noticeably less bright.

Colour light signals are capable of displaying more speed indications than semaphores, either by means of a subsidiary speed indicator or a variable speed sign. In the latter case, only the lower of the current and next speeds will be displayed; to differentiate between the two, the distant indication flashes.

All indications and speeds given by the lineside signals may be overridden by the cab signalling systems; the limits specified are for trains without operable cab signalling equipment.

Home Signals

Home signals protect the entrance to stations and junctions, and are indicated by a square red-yellow chequered post plate and a square-ended semaphore arm. They are absolute, so when at stop they cannot be passed without special permission from the signal box. Every station has at least one home signal; some stations may have more than one home signal for the same direction. In this case, the home signals are numbered in descending order from the first home signal.

Home signals that protect diverging routes are capable of displaying variable speed aspects; the 45° aspect indicates a diverging route, instead of caution as is normally the case. Home signals with two arms act as a combined distant signal for the next main signal.

There are also Combined Home signals which are capable of displaying aspects of both home and distant signals. These differ from the twin-armed semaphore signals in that they have an additional light to indicate high speed through route aspects.

Distant Signals

Distant signals give an advance warning of the next home (and sometimes block) signal, and are indicated with a hexagonal yellow-and-black post plate and a yellow fishtail semaphore arm. Most distant signals also have a green marker light above the arm; this is a historical feature predating the introduction of the yellow light.

They are used whenever the distance between home or block signals is greater than approximately 1800 metres, and are nearly always automatic signals, operating in conjunction with their associated main signal. Unlike home signals, distant signals cannot enforce a speed restriction, however they can advise of a speed restriction at the next signal. Distant signals capable of displaying a speed execution indication are always used as repeating signals, except in very rare cases.

Block Signals

Block signals are automatic and cannot give multiple speed indications. They are normally only located on the open line between stations, and are often permissive. They are identified by a yellow post plate with a small red circle in its centre, or a pointed semaphore arm.

Many block signals can also act as distant signals for the next main signal; for this role they have a lower yellow light. On the earliest designs of automatic block semaphore, a reflector was used to produce the second light for the Caution indication.

The four-light variant was introduced to allow block signals to display an Approach Medium indication, used where the speed signalling system optimises the distance between following trains.

Signal Aspects

The aspects are given for all variants of each type of signal in use.

Whenever a train is governed by both a lineside signal and a cab signal, the cab signal has higher precedence.

Stop

Driver must stop before the signal, and cannot pass it until the signal changes to a less restrictive indication, or is given a special order to proceed. Automatic block signals may permit passing of this signal at a restricted speed after waiting for 3 minutes.

Proceed Through

Driver can proceed not exceeding 84 km/h and expected the line to be clear until the next signal, which will also be displaying a Proceed indication. Unlike Proceed, this aspect guarantees that the next signal is displaying a Proceed indication; the former aspect only implies that the next signal is not at Stop. This indication is only given at the entrance to block sections in which there are no turnouts. When this aspect is displayed by a signal acting as a Distant signal for a station, it indicates that the straight (i.e. non-diverging) route through the station is set, and that all signals in the station area are displaying Proceed.
