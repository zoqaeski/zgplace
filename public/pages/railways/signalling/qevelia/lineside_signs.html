Qevelian Railway Signalling

Lineside Signs

There are a variety of static signs located along the right-of-way that convey information to the driver.

Signal-related signs

Approach markers

Approach markers are placed approximately 300, 200 and 100 metres from signals on the open line. Up to five signs may be provided where visibility of the signal is limited. When line speed is 60 km/h or less, one marker is sufficient.

Main signal approach

Approach markers for main signals are normally only provided where visibility of the signal is restricted, or when the signal is not located in the normal location.

Signal location boards

This sign indicates that a signal is not in its normal location to the left of the track. The sign is placed where the signal would be located. A secondary marker sign may be provided that points towards the signal for which this sign is applicable.

Post plates

Signal types are identified by signs mounted on the post, as the same indication may be given by a variety of different signals. Post plates come in full and half-size varieties, used to display up to two plates on a signal at once. There are four types of signal identified by post plates.

Home signals

Home signal post plates consist of alternating red and yellow stripes.

Distant signals

These are identified by a yellow/black striped plate. The give an advance indication of the following home or block signal.

Block signals

Block signals are identified by a yellow/red/yellow striped plate.

Combined signals

These signals have two post plates: a home signal plate above a yellow triangle. Combined signals are home signals that can display distant aspects.

Shunting signals

These are identified with a variety of blue/white striped plates:

  • Blue/white/blue plates mark signals only valid for shunting movements;
  • Red/white/blue plates mark a shunting signal that is also valid for train movements, but not capable of displaying a less restrictive aspect than Calling-On;
  • A main signal capable of displaying Proceed will have two post plates, a half-size Home plate and a half-size Shunting plate. If the main signal is combined with a distant, a half-size blue/white/red plate is used instead.

Signs in stations

Landmark signs

Landmark signs indicate the presence of upcoming stations that are unsignalled or do not have distant signals. They function as a permanent distant set at Expect Stop, and are placed at braking distance from the station limits.

Station Limit signs

Station limit signs define the geographical limits of a station. I have not drawn these yet as I’m not sure if I need them.

Shunt Limit signs

Shunt limit signs define the shunting limits of a location. Shunting movements are not permitted to pass this sign unless issued with a written Shunt Ahead order. They are located at the overlap distance from the Accepting signal. Stations where shunting frequently takes place on the main tracks may have an entry block outside the Accepting signal to permit shunting movements to proceed further up the line.

Stop signs

Stop signs indicate the end of a locked entry route and must not be passed unless authorised. These signs are also placed on buffer stops.

Stopping marker boards

These signs indicate the position at which trains of the specified length should stop. They are normally numbered in multiples of four chains (~100 metres); trains which do not fit into the 100-metre lengths should stop at the next marker board. When an ATC balise group is located beyond the stopping point, a blue sign with white text is used instead, indicating that a train with active cab signals must not pass this point to avoid fouling the balise group.

Signs for Electric Operation

Most railways in Qevelia are electrified; at present electrification accounts for approximately 85–90% of the route mileage. Most lines are powered by overhead catenary energised with alternating current at 16 kV 24 Hz; the low frequency is due to historical technological limitations. Despite improvements in technology, there have been no widespread adoptions of industrial-frequency (60 Hz) electrification, as there are few real advantages to doing so. There are a range of signals and signs in use which communicate to the driver the state of the electrical system.

Other signs

Signs indicating grades

Changes in the gradient are indicated by signs which display the inclination, whether it is ascending or descending, and the length of the grade. The inclination is in parts per 1728 (note that this is 1000 in duodecimal), and the length is in metres.

Sign Indication Meaning
Start or change of gradient Here: Line climbs a 16/1728 grade (~9.25‰) for the next 566 metres.
Start of horizontal section Here: Line is level for the next 5596 metres.
Start or change of falling gradient Here: Line falls at 45/1728 (~26‰) for the next 2678 metres.

Mileposts

Railway directions are usually described as “up” and “down”, with up being towards a major location. Throughout most of the network, up is the direction towards Kirua; for those lines that do not head towards Kirua, up is the direction towards the coast or the centre of an operating division.

Mileposts (as well as signals, turnouts, and other items of infrastructure) are numbered in the down direction. They are located approximately every 308 metres, or quarter of a mile; the upper figure is the mile, and the lower indicates subdivisions of the mile, in chains (~26 m) (and metres if more precision is required).
