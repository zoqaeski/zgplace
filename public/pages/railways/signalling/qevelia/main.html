Qevelian Railway Signalling

Main and Distant Signals

Main signals protect areas used by train movements, such as stations and the open line. Generally their aspects are not applicable to shunting movements, especially when the shunting signal gives a conflicting indication.

Designs

There are a number of classifications of signals, although unlike some systems the signalling rulebook does not distinguish between controlled and automatic signals. All controlled signals are interlocked with their associated turnout routes, and automatic signals are, by definition, interlocked with their associated block sections.

All semaphore signals are three-position (though not all positions may be operable) and operate in the upper-left quadrant. The earlier designs were worked by the double-wire system in preference to rod-worked or single-wire mechanisms, as it permits operation in three positions over significantly longer distances. Electro-pneumatic and later relay-based interlocking introduced power signalling, operated by compressed air, or more frequently, electric motors. The first automatic block semaphores used motors and relays that imitated the operation of the manual block system.

Colour-light signals were first introduced during electrification of the main lines and later the suburban railway networks of Kirua and Deşva. The aspects are a the same as the night aspects of the semaphore signals. To prevent failure of a bulb resulting in a wrong-side failure, all bulbs have a double filament which is automatically lit if the main one burns out. This filament is slightly out-of-focus so a burnt-out light will appear noticeably less bright.

All indications and speeds given by the lineside signals may be overridden by the cab signalling systems; the limits specified are for trains without operable cab signalling equipment.

Main Signals

Main signals protect the entrance to stations and junctions, and are indicated by a red-yellow-red striped post plate and a square-ended semaphore arm. They are absolute, so when at stop they cannot be passed without special permission from the signal box. Every station has at least one main signal; some stations may have more than one main signal for the same direction. In this case, the main signals are numbered in descending order from the first main signal.

Main signals that protect diverging routes are capable of displaying variable speed aspects; the 45° aspect indicates a medium speed route, instead of caution as is normally the case.

Distant Signals

Distant signals give an advance warning of the next main (and sometimes Block) signal, and are indicated by a yellow fishtail semaphore arm, and a yellow-black-yellow striped post plate. They always display two lights, to distinguish them from main signal aspects by night. They are located at between 400 to 2000 metres from the main signal they protect; 1200 metres is the standardised distance, as this is the braking distance for a passenger train with strong brakes from 160 km/h.

Reduced-distance indication

If the distance between the distant signal and the main signal it protects is less than 1200 metres, a lunar white light is displayed below the distant signal head. This light is not used with semaphore distant signals.

Repeater Signals

Repeater signals are a type of distant signal used where the visibility of a main signal may be restricted. Unlike distant signals, repeater signals do not have post plates. They are identified by lunar white light is displayed above the distant signal head.

A dwarf version is also available for use in areas with restricted clearance.

An older style consists of a position-light array of lunar white lights that emulates the position of a semaphore arm. These signals are being replaced with the modified distant signal as above.

Combined Main and Distant signals

If the distance between main signals is less than 1500 metres, a distant signal may be mounted on the same mast. Such combined signals are normally used as the accepting signals for stations, as the starting signal will be within the 1200 metre distance. The two-unit combined signals are only capable of displaying either a main or a distant aspect at any particular time, but not both at the same time.

A two-unit combined signal in which the main and distant aspects are merged was also developed. These replaced block signals at automatic block posts and remotely controlled stations, but are not used where both main and distant speed limitations need to be announced. The recent introduction of illuminated speed indicators has altered this practise to some extent, but have not seen widespread use except in some busy main line sections (for “train speed optimisation”) and at lengthy “passing lanes” installed as a temporary measure from complete duplication, triplication, or further amplification of tracks.

Combined signals with variable speed indicators (if multiple speed limits are required) are being installed in all new resignalling and upgrading projects, as these are a much more flexible system, capable of displaying aspects permitting train speed optimisation.

Block Signals

Block signals are automatic and cannot give multiple speed indications. They are normally only located on the open line between stations, and may be permissive. They are identified by a yellow post plate with narrow red stripe at its centre, or a pointed semaphore arm.

Many block signals can also act as distant signals for the next main signal; for this role they will display two lights. Where block signals are located closer than braking distance, they are fitted with a lunar white repeating light that is lit when the aspect displayed by the signal in rear is more restrictive.

Signal Aspects

The aspects are given for all variants of each type of signal in use.

Whenever a train is governed by both a lineside signal and a cab signal, the cab signal has higher precedence.

Expect Stop

Driver must be prepared to stop before the next signal. As distant signals are placed at braking distance from the main signal, the driver should start reducing speed immediately upon passing this signal.

Expect Medium

Driver must reduce to Medium speed (36 km/h if no indication) before the next signal. A speed other than 36 km/h is indicated by either pulsating lights or a fixed or variable Medium Speed sign. The medium speed specified by the signal is the minimum speed that the next signal may display.

It is interesting to note that a mechanical signal failure would result in a right-side failure, but a failure of the colour light signal could result in a wrong-side failure. To guard against this, the lights are wired in series such that a failure of one bulb will result in a dark signal.

To provide for 60 km/h and higher speed turnouts, an additional aspect was introduced by pulsating the lights.

Expect High-Speed

This aspect was introduced to provide a distant indication of a high-speed diverging route for speeds greater than 100 km/h. It announces a speed restriction to a minimum of 100 km/h; other speed indications will be given by an illuminated speed sign.
