Qevelian Railway Signalling

Main Signals

Main signals protect areas used by train movements, such as stations and the open line. Generally their aspects are not applicable to shunting movements, especially when the shunting signal gives a conflicting indication.

Designs

There are a number of classifications of signals, although unlike some systems the signalling rulebook does not distinguish between controlled and automatic signals. All controlled signals are interlocked with their associated turnout routes, and automatic signals are, by definition, interlocked with their associated block sections.

All semaphore signals are three-position (though not all positions may be operable) and operate in the upper-left quadrant. The earlier designs were worked by the double-wire system in preference to rod-worked or single-wire mechanisms, as it permits operation in three positions over significantly longer distances. Electro-pneumatic and later relay-based interlocking introduced power signalling, operated by compressed air, or more frequently, electric motors. The first automatic block semaphores used motors and relays that imitated the operation of the manual block system.

Colour-light signals were first introduced during electrification of the main lines and later the suburban railway networks of Kirua and Deşva. The aspects are a the same as the night aspects of the semaphore signals. To prevent failure of a bulb resulting in a wrong-side failure, all bulbs have a double filament which is automatically lit if the main one burns out. This filament is slightly out-of-focus so a burnt-out light will appear noticeably less bright.

All indications and speeds given by the lineside signals may be overridden by the cab signalling systems; the limits specified are for trains without operable cab signalling equipment.

Home Signals

Home signals protect stations, junctions and other points of a physical obstruction or conflict. They are indicated by a red-yellow-red striped post plate and a square-ended semaphore arm. Every station has at least one home signal; some stations may have more than one home signal for the same direction.

Home signals that protect diverging routes are capable of displaying variable speed aspects; the 45° aspect indicates a medium speed route, instead of caution as is normally the case.

Distant Signals

Distant signals give an advance warning of the next home (and sometimes block) signal, and are indicated by a yellow fishtail semaphore arm, and a yellow-black-yellow striped post plate. They always display two lights, to distinguish them from home signal aspects by night. They are located at between 400 to 2000 metres from the home signal they protect; 1200 metres is the standardised distance, as this is the braking distance for a passenger train with strong brakes from 160 km/h.

Repeating Signals

Repeating signals are a type of distant signal used where the visibility of a main signal may be restricted. They consist of a position-light array of lunar white lights that emulates the position of a semaphore arm. Unlike ordinary distant signals, repeaters do not have post plates.

A dwarf version of the repeater signal is used where there are restricted clearances.

Combined Home and Distant signals

If the distance between home signals is less than 1500 metres, a distant signal may be mounted on the same mast. Such combined signals are normally used as the Accepting signals for stations, as the Starting signal will be within the 1200 metre distance.

A two-unit combined signal in which the home and distant aspects are merged was also developed. These replaced block signals at automatic block posts and remotely controlled stations, but are not used where both home and distant speed limitations need to be announced. The recent introduction of illuminated speed indicators has altered this practise to some extent, but have not seen widespread use except in some busy main line sections (for “train speed optimisation”) and at lengthy “passing lanes” installed as a temporary measure from complete duplication, triplication, or further amplification of tracks.

Block Signals

Block signals are located on the open line between stations, and are used to divide the line into sections. They are identified by a yellow post plate with narrow red stripe at its centre, or a pointed semaphore arm.

Most block signals are automatic, and cannot display aspects which enforce a reduced speed, except on densely trafficked sections with blocks shorter than 800 metres. The typical block length is a little over 1200 metres; blocks longer than 1800 metres will generally use a two-aspect system with separate distant signals.

Signal Aspects

The aspects are given for all variants of each type of signal in use.

Whenever a train is governed by both a lineside signal and a cab signal, the cab signal has higher precedence.

Expect Stop

Driver must be prepared to stop before the next signal. As distant signals are placed at braking distance from the home signal, the driver should start reducing speed immediately upon passing this signal.

Expect Medium

Driver must reduce to Medium speed (36 km/h if no indication) before the next signal. A speed other than 36 km/h is indicated by either pulsating lights or a fixed or variable Medium Speed sign. The medium speed specified by the signal is the minimum speed that the next signal may display.

It is interesting to note that a mechanical signal failure would result in a right-side failure, but a failure of the colour light signal could result in a wrong-side failure. To guard against this, the lights are wired in series such that a failure of one bulb will result in a dark signal.

To provide for 60 km/h and higher speed turnouts, an additional aspect was introduced by pulsating the lights.
