Qevesa Grammar

Introduction

Preface

This is a reference grammar of Qevesa, a Northwestern Teralo language. Specifically, this grammar deals with the standard dialect as spoken on the west coast.

The name of the language

The language described here is known to native speakers as well as outsiders as Qevesa. In Qevesa the language may also be referred to as Qeveli ki-sani, ‘language of the Qeveli’, or yevaik sani, ‘our language’. The word qevesa is itself an archaic term meaning ‘Qeveli speech’.

Demography

Qevesa is spoken today by approximately 18 million speakers, of which there are about 16 million in the Republic of Qevelia, about 900 000 in the United Kingdoms of Semati, and a further 200 000 in spread across central and south-western Teranië. As travel abroad is accessible, approximately a quarter of the population are polyglots. Furthermore, there are many multilingual speakers of minority languages spoken in some of the mountainous provinces.

Dialects

Qevesa is a Northwestern Teralo language, along with Keřesai and Voðirza. The current period, Modern Qevesa, started around the year 1200KE, following Middle Qevesa, which reached from 750KE to 1200KE. Prior to 750KE, Classical Qevesa (Këvesa) was spoken across the Kovask Empire. The modern standard (literary) language is based on the dialects spoken on the west coast of Qevelia; however the spoken language has a number of dialects, which can be broadly divided into two groups, Western Qevesa and Eastern Qevesa.

The diphthongs that arise from the vowels are /ai au æi æy ei oi ou øi øy/, as well as /i-/ and /u-/ glides, which are realised as [w-] and [j-] respectively. Length is distinguished only where two adjacent vowels are the same, or when a vowel precedes /h/. Other vowel pairs occur at syllable boundaries and are not diphthongs.

Syllable Structure

Syllables can begin and end with any phoneme in the consonant inventory, or optionally none at all. Clusters of consonants can only occur at the syllable boundaries, with the exception of affricates and consonant–glide combinations (i.e. those that consist of consonant + /j/ or consonant + /w/). Words are divided into syllables according to the rule that non-word-final consonants are always the onset of syllables unless followed by another consonant. This leads to the following basic scheme:

  • (C)V(C) is the basic syllable. V represents either a singular vowel or a diphthong.
  • CVCVC is realised as CV|CVC
  • VCVC is realised as V|CVC
  • VCCVC is realised as VC|CVC
  • CVVC is realised as CV|VC

However, there are (slight) exceptions to this rule:

  • geminated consonants may occur at a syllable coda, although these are in practice followed by an unvoiced vowel, typically /i/

Prosody

The Verb Complex

Qevesa is a language based on verbal triliteral roots, so the verb is the most complex and most important category. Verbs are marked for topical agreement, aspect and mood; other constructions are marked periphrastically.

Conjugation

A Qevesa verb has eleven principal forms, corresponding to the grammatical aspects and the citation form, which is a non-finite form. Excluding verbal prefixes, a single verb can easily have over 11,000 forms — with a single prefix, over 40,000. These forms are comprised of the following affixes and clitics, in this order:
stem.aspect-subject-mood

Triliteral Root Classes

Qevesa is a language based on triliteral roots; a large percentage of the lexicon is derived from a considerably smaller base set of consonant groups. The system is highly regular, with the only irregularities occurring with certain consonants, especially /h/ and /j/, which are often lenited or otherwise omitted. This causes the triliteral roots to be divided into the following classes:

  • Class I roots do not contain -h-, in any position other than the initial. This is the largest group of triliteral roots.
  • Class II include all biliteral roots.
  • Class III roots are those which have -h- as the second consonant.
  • Class IV roots are those which have -h- as the third consonant. These also act as biliteral roots in many cases.

The citation form of each of these classes is as follows:

There are also irregular verbs which could be categorised into more than one class, but these are very rare.

Quadriliteral Roots

There are a small number of quadriliteral roots, usually formed by derivations and compounding. These roots may be categorised as any of the four classes, but are usually grouped in Class I, depending on the specific consonants that comprise the root. As only two-consonant clusters that fall on a syllable break are permissable, the position of the consonants relative to the transfix vowels varies to satisfy the phonological rules.

Aspect

Aspect is possibly the most important grammatical category marked on the verb. Instead of tense, aspect is used to mark the temporal flow (or lack thereof) of verbs. Qevesa distinguishes between imperfective aspects (those that are ongoing, habitual, repeated or generally containing internal structure) and perfective aspects (those that are viewed as a single whole). There are two primary transfix patterns, corresponding to the imperfective and perfective aspects, and a number of secondary transfix patterns which indicate various subtle (mainly semantic) differences.

The Imperfective Aspects

The imperfective aspects are used to indicate the following:

  • actions in progress, just ongoing states and activities, with significant course (in opinion of the speaker);
  • activities posing the background for other (perfective) activities;
  • simultaneous activities;
  • durative activities, lasting through some time;
  • multiple (iterative or frequentative) activities;
  • motions without a strict aim;
  • non-resultative activities, only heading towards some purpose;
  • continuous states

These are marked as follows:

The Perfective Aspects

The perfective aspects generally indicate activities that have distinct beginnings and ends which are relevant to the speaker. This implies past or future activities, but not present activities – an activity which is happening now cannot be ended, so it cannot be perfective. The perfective indicates the following:

  • states and activities which were ended (even if a second ago) or which will be ended, with insignificant course, or treated as a whole by the speaker;
  • single-time activities;
  • actions whose goals have already been achieved;
  • reasons for the state;
  • the beginning of the activity or the state;
  • the end of the activity or the state;
  • activities executed in many places, on many objects or by many subjects at the same time;
  • actions or states which last some time

The Copula

The copula as an independent verb does not exist. Generally, in most cases where a copula would be used, the verb is instead conjugated to the stative imperfective aspect.

The Noun Phrase

Basic Noun Syntax

The structure of a Qevesa noun phrase is relatively rigid. The order of the constituent elements is det-rel-adj-noun-post (determiner, relative clauses, adjectives, noun, postpositional phrases).

Proper Names

Names are treated differently from ordinary nouns in that they do not usually receive a suffixed case marker. Names are also not usually marked for number.

Case

Case markers in Qevesa are suffixes that attach to the head noun of a noun phrase. There are 14 cases in total, divided into two groups of five and nine members. The former group is referred to as the primary cases; these mark important grammatical elements such as the topic, the agent, the patient, and the theme of the verb. The other cases are the secondary cases, which mark location, time, and other similar information. An exception to the above is the genetive case, which is in the primary group despite being seldom used.

Most nouns are countable and can be marked for number with a suffix that comes between the stem and the case marker. Qevesa possesses three numbers, singular, plural and quantitative. Pronouns

Pronouns are roughly equivalent to nouns in terms of syntax and morphology, although they tend to have some irregular declensions.

Personal Pronouns

Most pronouns refer only to animate referents; a separate inanimate pronoun is used for inanimate referents. Pronouns are entirely without gender, and make no disctinction between male/female/neuter. There are two first person plural pronouns, an inclusive, which includes the listener, and an exclusive, which does not.

Personal pronouns can only be declined to the primary cases in the ordinary manner (i.e. with suffixed case markers). Although a genetive form of the personal pronouns exists, it is rarely used; instead, the suffix form is preferred.

Articles

Qevesa lacks articles for nouns. Determiners may be used to perform a similar purpose, but this is uncommon.

It can be seen that numbers are extremely head-marking. However this introduces ambiguities, in that the pronunciation of 203,000₁₂, for example, is the same as 200,003₁₂: *ëtossińa-köl. In order to avoid this problem, the use of u (“and”) is obligatory in such situations: ëtossińa-köl (203,000₁₂) and ëtossińa u köl.

Derivational Morphology

Qevesa is based on triliteral roots, so a large proportion of the lexicon is produced from a much smaller set of roots. Most nouns and modifiers are in fact derived from verb forms.

Derivation of new roots

There are a number of patterns which can create additional roots from the verb triliteral base.

The Causative

In addition to being a modal suffix, the causative also exists as a special verb form that extends the triliteral patterns and classes. A causative verb formed by this process often has the second and third consonants of the original triliteral root grouped together to make a quadriliteral root. Patterns that group root consonants together generally insert the causative -k- after the third root consonant, but this varies depending on the root and is the only consistently irregular aspect in Qevesa.
