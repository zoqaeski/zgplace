Allophonic Variations

Most phonemes are pronounced as per the table above, with few variations. However, the following allophones do exist:

  • [ʋ] may be realised as any of the following: [v], [f], [β], [ɸ], [β̞]
  • Voicing in clusters assimilates to the normal realisation of the final consonant.

Stress and Prosody

The concept of stress is very different in Qevesa as compared to English. Stress nearly always falls on the penultimate syllable of a polysyllabic word; monosyllabic words are unstressed. There are two forms of intonation; relative intonation and overall intonation. Relative intonation tends to roughly follow stress patterns on a syllable basis, with stressed syllables pronounced at a higher tone. Overall intonation is mainly syntactical, rising for questions and exclamations, and remaining flat for most statements.

Syllables consist of an onset, a nucleus and a coda, the onset and coda being optional.

Onset:
any single consonant;
any affricate;
any plosive or nasal + /j/
/s/ or /ɕ/ + any unvoiced plosive or affricate, any nasal
Any plosive or /s ɕ/ + /ʋ/
/p t k/ + /s ɕ/
/p k b g/ + /r/
Nucleus:
any vowel or diphthong.
The Coda:
any single consonant except for /j/;
any affricate;
/s ɕ r/ + any unvoiced plosive or affricate;
any nasal or /r/ + /s ɕ/ and/or any affricate.

Syllable divisions occur before a single consonant or between two consonants which do not form an acceptable onset cluster. Divisions also occur between vowels which do not form diphthongs.

Orthography

Qevesa is normally written using the kirna alphabet. However, for the purpose of this document a Latin transliteration is used, which matches one-to-one to the kirna script. Whilst it may have been more convenient to use a Latin orthography matched phonetically, the use of the system employed allows easier transcription into kirnagul, because Qevesa spelling is not entirely phonetic, with some phonemes having more than one orthographical representation. Attempts at an orthography reform have not met the success that the standard Qevesa dialect did; in hindsight it was probably an unwise idea to ceate a standard language without standardising the orthography at the same time.

Capitalisation and Acronyms

Capitalisation in Qevesa is completely regular. Only the first letter of a sentence and first letter of a proper noun are capitalised (regardless of grammatical case).

  • Qevelia (p.n.) Qevelia
  • Qevelëk (p.n. GEN / p.adj) Qevelian (lit. "of Qevelia")
  • qeveli (c.adj) typical of Qevelia or Qevelians
  • qevela (c.n. a Qevelian person)

Acronyms are written with the capitalised first letter of each component word, and any following letters from the same word are lowercase. There is no punctuation between letters of an acronym, but they are usually followed by a full stop. Acronyms which are themselves loan terms are not usually translated.

  • QŽlK. (Qevelëk Ževkilužegik Keltova, Qevelian Office of Foreign Affairs)
  • QMq. (Qevelëk Meskiqelava, Qevelian Television)

When acronyms or abbreviations are used as words, a colon is placed between them and any inflections or conjugations.

AÉL:is Jovo qelukmir!
UFO-ACC John-DCHT see-3sg.IPF
John saw a UFO!

Punctuation

Formatting for full stops, commas, colons, exclamation/question marks, etc. is the same as in English with no space after the preceding word.

Single quotation marks are used to indicate emphasis on a word or phrase in writing, which in speech, would be indicated with a change in tone.

Guillemets (angle quotes, « ») are used to indicate speech in a similar manner to English, although with the following differences:

  • Commas are never used before or after the guillemets to mark interuptions (they serve this purpose as well);
  • Guillemets are always used to indicate speech, even paraphrased speech.

Quotations that span multiple paragraphs are block-quoted, with a left pointing guillemet at the start of the quotation and a right pointing guillemet at the end of the quotation.

Colons have an extra use not present in English: as an optional marker to separate acronyms and loan words from conjugations. The use with loan words is very rare, as most such words are usually naturalised by adopting to the standard Qevesa orthography.

Indentation is always used in written Qevesa to start a new paragraph, list entry, and heading (unless it is centre aligned).
