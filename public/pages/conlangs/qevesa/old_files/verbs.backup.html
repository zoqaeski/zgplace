Qevesa Grammar

Verbal Morphology

The verb is the most complex part of Qevesa, and the most important element of a sentence. Nearly all verbs have two parts, a stem that is marked for polypersonal agreement, and an auxiliary verb that is marked for aspect, mood and evidentiality.

The Verb Stem

The verb stem is usually non-finite (indeed, only a handful of irregular verbs have finite forms) and only marks polypersonal agreement with the subject and object(s). It normally consists of C1uC2C3u. Affixes are inserted into P0 (before C1), P23 (between C2 and C3) and replacing P4 (after C3) to indicate person; no other conjugations are marked directly on the verb stem (ignoring irregular verbs for the moment).

Morphosyntactic alignment

This section is detailed in syntax, but to get an introduction to the verbal system of Qevesa it is briefly described here. Qevesa is a fluid-S language, whose primary morphological cases are outlined as follows:

  • The Subject of an involuntary (i.e. subject not in control) intransitive verb is marked with the same case as the Theme of a ditransitive verb.
  • The Subject of a voluntary (i.e. subject in control) intransitive verb is marked with the same case as the Agent of a transitive verb or Donor of a ditransitive verb.
  • The Patient of a transitive verb is always marked with the same case as the Recipient of a ditransitive verb.

These three primary cases are referred to as the secundative case, the nominative case and the primative case. Each of these primary cases forms a verb paradigm in the triconsonantal system.

The Three Verb Paradigms

The Secundative Paradigm

This is only used with intransitive and ditransitive verbs, to indicate the involuntary subject and the theme. When used alone, the involuntary intransitive is implied; elsewhere the verb must be ditransitive. It is formed by the addition of a affix in P4. If not marked, P4 consists of the vowel -u.

1sg -e
2sg -a
3sg -ō
1pl -ev
2pl -av
3pl -ōv
3;inanim -is

The Nominative Paradigm

The nominative may be used with intransitive, (mono-)transitive and ditransitive verbs. When used alone, the voluntary intransitive is implied; combinining with other paradigms produces transitive and ditransitive verbs. It is formed by the insertion of an infix in P23, which replaces any other vowels there.

1sg -e-
2sg -a-
3sg -ō-
1pl -ev-
2pl -av-
3pl -ōv-
3;inanim -ī-

The Primative Paradigm

Unlike the other two paradigms, the primative cannot be used alone, as it marks the patient or the recipient of a transitive or ditransitive verb. It is formed by the insertion of a prefix in P0.

2sg jā-
3sg ō-
1pl ēv-
2pl jav-
3pl jov-
3;inanim ī-

From the above tables, the following can be noted about all three paradigms:

  • The basic infixes are:
    • 1sg: (-)e(-)
    • 2sg: (-)a(-)
    • 3sg: (-)ō(-)
    • 3;inanim: (-)is(-)
  • The plural is indicated by a -v- that follows the person-marking vowel, however this never occurs for the inanimate third person.

The Auxiliary Verb

The auxiliary verb is used to indicate everything else, and is mandatory in all but informal speech. Categories marked on the auxiliary include aspect, mood and evidentiality, and optionally voice, and polarity. Qevesa does not mark tense at all; if time needs to be indicated, an adverb will be used instead. The auxiliary verb agrees with its head (the stem verb) in transivity.

Aspect

Instead of tense, aspect is used to mark the temporal flow (or lack thereof) of verbs. Qevesa distinguishes between imperfective aspects (those that are ongoing, habitual, repeated or generally containing internal structure) and perfective aspects (those that are viewed as a single whole). Each of these uses a different auxiliary verb construction.

The Imperfective Aspects

The imperfective aspects are used to indicate the following:

  • progressive or durative actions (the dynamic imperfective);
  • states and activities posing the background for other (perfective) activities (the background imperfective);
  • simultaneous activities (the simultaneous imperfective);
  • habitual activities (the habitual imperfective);
  • multiple (iterative) activities (the iterative imperfective);
  • non-resultative activities (the irresultative imperfective); and
  • continuous states (the stative imperfective).

The imperfect auxiliary verb stem is da.

From the table above, the following generalities can be observed:

  • d-l- is intransitive;
  • d-s- is transitive;
  • d-c- is ditransitive;

The Perfective Aspects

The perfective aspects generally indicate activities that have distinct beginnings and ends which are relevant to the speaker. This implies past or future activities, but not present activities – an activity which is happening now cannot be ended, so it cannot be perfective. The perfective indicates the following:

  • states and activities which were ended (even if a second ago) or which will be ended (the simple perfective);
  • single-time activities (the singulative);
  • actions whose goals have already been achieved, especially those viewed from the perspective of a later time (the retrospective);
  • the beginning of the activity or the state (the inceptive);
  • the end of the activity or the state (the terminative); and
  • actions or states which last some time but have a definite end (the durative perfective).

The perfective auxiliary verb stem is ko.

Mood

Qevesa indicates mood through suffixes on the auxiliary verb, some of which cause changes in its final vowel. At least one mood is mandatory.

The Declarative Mood

The declarative mood is equivalent to the indicative mood, and indicates that the statement is true. It is formed by the addition of the suffix -u, although this is usually merged with the final vowel of the auxiliary verb as follows:

a → au
i y
ī ȳ
o ō
u ū
y ȳ

The Conditional Mood

The conditional mood is used to refer to an action or state that is dependent on a condition, and to refer to that condition. It is formed by the addition of the suffix -iti. The following auxiliary vowel changes occur:

a → ai
i ī
ī ī
o oi
u ui
y ȳ

The Intentive Mood

The intentive mood is used to indicate that the speaker intends to perform the action. It is formed by the addition of the suffix -qi.

The Optative Mood

The optative mood is used to indicate wishes and desires. It is formed by the suffix -ši.

The Prohibitive Mood

The prohibitive mood indicate that the action or state cannot occur. It is formed by the suffix -me.

The Imperative Mood

The imperative mood is used to indicate orders and commands. It is formed by the suffix -ːl; i.e. lengthening of the previous vowel and the addition of -l; long vowels are unmodified.

Evidentiality

The final mandatory part of the auxiliary verb is the evidentiality suffix. There are four degrees of evidentiality:

Witness
The speaker is a witness to the action or event, so possesses first-hand knowledge.
Inferential
The speaker infers based on other evidence, usually physical.
Reportative
Used for reported statements or hearsay.
Assumption
Used for assumptions without evidence.
Witness ewts -m
Inferential einf -k
Reportative erpt -z
Assumption easm -t

Other Auxiliary Constructions

The auxiliary verb has a number of regular constructions that are used to convey additional information.

The Causative

The causative is in a class of its own, in that it is used so frequently that it is likely to become a regular suffix. Derived from ispurōku kozym (s/he causes it), it consists of the enclitic poik.

Polarity

Polarity is an unusual development of the negative form of verbs, in which the negation suffix detached from the auxiliary verb, possibly by analogy with the affirmative particle tō. The negative particle is mō. These particles can also occur with nouns and adjectives, although in those cases they act as proclitics instead or enclitic particles.

Nominalisations

As Qevesa is based on triconsonantal roots, most nouns are formed from verb roots, and each root can produce a variety of nominal forms.

Concept

CeCeC

This is a general nominalisation representing the concept, process, activity or ability.

Actor

CaCoC

A person that performs the action.

Patient

CoCCit

Something that undergoes the action.

Resulting Object

CiCāC

An object resulting from the action.

Art

CeCöCa

Names an art, skill, or science.

Device

CeC~äC

A non-human object that performs the action.

Instrument

CīCuC

A tool or object used to perform the action.

Location

aCCēC

A place where the action is performed.

Gender specific variants of the above nominalisations can be formed with the suffixes -(i)ro and -(i)sse.
