Introduction

Preface

This is a reference grammar of Qevesa, a Northwestern Teralo language. Specifically, this grammar deals with the standard dialect as spoken on the west coast.

The name of the language

The language described here is known to native speakers as well as outsiders as Qevesa. In Qevesa the language may also be referred to as Qeveli ké-saní, ‘language of the Qeveli’, or ëvaik saní, ‘our language’. The word qevesa is itself an archaic term meaning ‘Qeveli speech’.

Demography

Qevesa is spoken today by approximately 7.7 million speakers, of which there are about 6.5 million in the Republic of Qevelia, about 900 000 in the United Kingdoms of Semati, and a further 200 000 in spread across central and south-western Teranië. As travel abroad is accessible, approximately a quarter of the population are polyglots. Furthermore, there are many multilingual speakers of minority languages spoken in some of the mountainous provinces.

Dialects

Qevesa is a Northwestern Teralo language, along with Keřesai and Vóðirza. The current period, Modern Qevesa, started around the year 1200KE, following Middle Qevesa, which reached from 750KE to 1200KE. Prior to 750KE, Classical Qevesa (Këvesa) was spoken across the Kovásk Empire. The modern standard (literary) language is based on the dialects spoken on the west coast of Qevelia; however the spoken language has a number of dialects, which can be broadly divided into two groups, Western Qevesa and Eastern Qevesa.

Syllable Structure

Syllables can begin and end with any phoneme in the consonant inventory, or optionally none at all. Clusters of consonants can only occur at the syllable boundaries, with the exception of affricates and consonant–glide combinations (i.e. those that consist of consonant + /j/ or consonant + /w/). Words are divided into syllables according to the rule that non-word-final consonants are always the onset of syllables unless followed by another consonant. This leads to the following basic scheme:

  • (C)V(C) is the basic syllable.
  • CVCVC is realised as CV|CVC
  • VCVC is realised as V|CVC
  • VCCVC is realised as VC|CVC

Morphology

Verbs

Qevesa is a language based on verbal triliteral roots, so the verb is the most complex and most important category. Verbs are marked for topical agreement, aspect, mood and evidentiality; other constructions are marked periphrastically.

Conjugation

A Qevesa verb has eleven principal forms, corresponding to the grammatical aspects and the citation form, which is a non-finite form. Excluding verbal prefixes, a single verb can easily have over 11,000 forms — with a single prefix, over 40,000. These forms are comprised of the following affixes and clitics, in this order:
polarity=stem.aspect-subject-mood-evidentiality

Triliteral Root Classes

Qevesa is a language based on triliteral roots; a large percentage of the lexicon is derived from a considerably smaller base set of consonant groups. The system is highly regular, with the only irregularities occurring with certain consonants, especially /h/ and /j/, which are often lenited or otherwise omitted. This causes the triliteral roots to be divided into the following classes:

  • Class I roots do not contain -h- or -j-, in any position. This is the largest group of triliteral roots.
  • Class II roots are those which have -h- as the second consonant. This class also includes nearly all biliteral roots.
  • Class III roots are those which have -h- as the third consonant.
  • Class IV roots are those which have -j- as the second or third consonant.
  • Class V verbs include all verbs whose roots have h- or j- as the initial consonant.

Aspect

Aspect is possibly the most important grammatical category marked on the verb. Instead of tense, aspect is used to mark the temporal flow (or lack thereof) of verbs. Qevesa distinguishes between imperfective aspects (those that are ongoing, habitual, repeated or generally containing internal structure) and perfective aspects (those that are viewed as a single whole). There are two primary transfix patterns, corresponding to the imperfective and perfective aspects, and a number of secondary transfix patterns which indicate various subtle (mainly semantic) differences.

The Imperfective Aspects

The imperfective aspects are used to indicate the following:

  • progressive or durative actions (the dynamic imperfective);
  • states and activities posing the background for other (perfective) activities (the background imperfective);
  • habitual activities (the habitual imperfective);
  • multiple (iterative) activities (the iterative imperfective);
  • non-resultative activities or states (the irresultative imperfective); and
  • continuous states (the stative imperfective).

The Perfective Aspects

The perfective aspects generally indicate activities that have distinct beginnings and ends which are relevant to the speaker. This implies past or future activities, but not present activities – an activity which is happening now cannot be ended, so it cannot be perfective. The perfective indicates the following:

  • states and activities which were ended (even if a second ago) or which will be ended (the simple perfective);
  • actions whose goals have already been achieved, especially those viewed from the perspective of a later time (the retrospective);
  • the beginning of the activity or the state (the inceptive);
  • the end of the activity or the state (the terminative); and

Mood

Qevesa indicates mood through suffixes on the verb, some of which cause changes in its final vowel. At least one mood is mandatory.

The Declarative Mood
The declarative mood is equivalent to the indicative mood, and indicates that the statement is true. It is formed by the addition of the suffix -u.
The Conditional Mood
The conditional mood is used to refer to an action or state that is dependent on a condition, and to refer to that condition. It is formed by the addition of the suffix -iti.
The Intentive Mood
The intentive mood is used to indicate that the speaker intends to perform the action. It is formed by the addition of the suffix -qi.
The Optative Mood
The optative mood is used to indicate wishes and desires. It is formed by the suffix -ši.
The Prohibitive Mood
The prohibitive mood indicate that the action or state cannot occur. It is formed by the suffix -me.
The Imperative Mood
The imperative mood is used to indicate orders and commands. It is formed by the suffix -ːl; i.e. lengthening of the previous vowel and the addition of -l; long vowels are unmodified.

Evidentiality

The final mandatory part of the verb is the evidentiality suffix. There are four degrees of evidentiality:

Witness
The speaker is a witness to the action or event, so possesses first-hand knowledge.
Inferential
The speaker infers based on other evidence, usually physical.
Reportative
Used for reported statements or hearsay.
Assumption
Used for assumptions without evidence.
Verb final
Witness ewts -vir
Inferential einf -kir
Reportative erpt -zir
Assumption easm -tir

Polarity

Polarity is an unusual development of the negative form of verbs, in which the negation suffix detached from the auxiliary verb, possibly by analogy with the affirmative proclitic tó.

Verbal Derivation

Verb prefixes and proclitics

There is a wide range of prefixes and proclitics which alter the meaning of a verb. Most of them are derivatives of other verbs or nouns, usually phonetic simplifications, although there are some which are independent lexemes. These can be grouped into several classes.

The Causative

The causative is a special construction which takes its own arguments. It consists of the proclitic pék.

Nouns

The declension of a Qevesa noun is generally quite regular; nouns are marked for case, number and possession. Other categories, such as gender, are seldom marked, although some nouns (such as pronouns and body parts) do possess so-called “natural gender”.

Proper Names

Names are treated differently from ordinary nouns in that they do not usually receive a suffixed case marker. Names are also not usually marked for number.

Most nouns are countable and can be marked for number with a suffix that comes between the stem and the case marker. Qevesa possesses three numbers, singular, plural and quantitative. Possession

Possession is normally marked by a proclitic particle, ké, unless the possessor is a pronoun, in which case the pronoun is declined to the genetive case.

Pronouns

Pronouns are roughly equivalent to nouns in terms of syntax and morphology, although they tend to have some irregular declensions.

Personal Pronouns

Most pronouns refer only to animate referents; a separate inanimate pronoun is used for inanimate referents. Pronouns are entirely without gender, and make no disctinction between male/female/neuter. There are two first person plural pronouns, an inclusive, which includes the listener, and an exclusive, which does not.

Articles

Qevesa has only the indefinite article, however it is used fairly infrequently.

The Indefinite Article

The indefinite article, sel, is a variant of the word for the numeral “one”, and as such is only used when referring to a single example of the head noun. A consequence of this is that the indefinite article is used with the quantitative plural form of the noun.

Modifiers

Adjectives and Adverbs

Qevesa distinguishes between attributive and predicative adjectives. The former follow the pattern CeCaCi and do not decline to case or agree with the noun, although they can be modified with the polarity proclitics. Predicative adjectives are essentially modified forms of the verb.

The attributive adjectives can be turned into adverbs by replacing the word final -i with -a and then casting it in the instrumental case. Like the attributive adjectives, adverbs can also be modified with the polarity proclitics.

  • Adjective: pelati beautiful
  • Adverb: tó-pelatátti beauty, with beauty
  • Negative adverb: mó-pelatátti without beauty

Adjectival Declension

Predicative adjectives decline in a similar manner to verbs, although most conjugation involves the use of suffixes rather than transfixes. Although only aspectual marking is shown here, a predicative adjective can be marked with all of the categories that are applicable to a verb.

Comparison

The Comparative Degree

The comparative degree is formed by placing the proclitic opóš before the adjective or adverb. The standard of comparison is formed by placing it in the comparative case.

A-a B-eňöc t’opóš-dülvéňonuvir.

A-a A-nom
B-eňöc. B-cmp
t=opóš=dülvé-ňon-u-vir aff=more=big.stv;ipfv-3;inanim.dct.top-decl-ewts

“A” is bigger than “B”.

The Superlative Degree

The superlative degree is formed by the circumfix o—eš, which is placed around the adjective or adverb.

A-a t’odülvéňonešuvir.

A-a A-nom
t=o>dülvé-ňon<eš-u-vir. aff=<most>big.stv;ipfv-3;inanim.dct.top-decl-ewts

“A” is the biggest.

The Dimunitive Degree

A dimunitive degree also exists, although is used far less often. It consists of the proclitic mëš or as the circumfix mi—eš. The proclitic is used for the dimunitive comparative, and the circumfix for the dimunitive superlative.

B-eňöc. B-cmp

“A” is not as big as “B”.

A-a A-nom
