Qeveli Culture: Government

The Republic of Qevelia (Qeveleqo Kiruba) has a totally different style of government compared to most terrestrial countries. The closest equivalent is democratic socialism, in which the democracy part is in fact a pure democracy, where every citizen is entitled to have a say in the running of things.

Some would say that Qevelia suffers from referendum disease of epidemic proportions, however they fail to take into account the scale of control that the people as a whole have over the running of their country.

National Divisions

The country is divided into 8 provinces (siepadriva), each roughly representing geographical regions. Each of these provinces is then divided into local government areas (valiva), each representing a town or city.

Every division of the state is run by a council of twelve, the toręa, of which one member is elected as an administrator by the populace of the division. There is no head of state, but a council of eight (yeřpa) jointly run the country.

Voting and Decisions

The administrators of each valai congregate in the capital city of the province to discuss the issues that affect their vala, and also coordinate the voting and decision making.

For every decision that is to be made by any division of the country the whole adult population affected by that decision must vote on the issue. The results of these votes are counted, and the majority in all levels is acted upon. The whole population is encouraged to take a part in the running of the country, and as such they may suggest ideas and proposals to their local administrators; in fact, once a year (around the budget time) a proposal sheet is sent to every voter, asking them to submit their ideas.

This collection of ideas is then sent up through the various levels, and roughly consistent trends are discussed by the various administrators to determine possible new policies. When finalised, they are voted upon by the public.

Every four years (offset by two years), a new administrator for each province and each valiva are chosen through a vote.

Role of the Government

The government, as such, is merely an administrative committee that sets down the laws, regulates the money, and so on, according to the peoples' choices as shown through the results of votes. A separate body, the stola, acts as an ombudsman to weed out corruption; in the case of such corruption being found, all concerned parties will be dissolved and votes re-held in the appropriate areas. This is required by the constitution, which was originally written to make the country a fair place.

All nationwide infrastructure is owned and regulated by the government; service providers are required by constitutional law to make providing a service their highest priority. An example of this is that the railway tracks are owned by the government, yet Qeveliqo Viažkoa (Qevelian Railways) is a publicly owned company. The limits on what services are provided are set by the government, which in turn is totally decided by the citizens of Qevelia.

The constitution states that all citizens of the country must have access to education, health care, social security, and other related services. These are paid for by the relatively high taxes, which are proportionally scaled so that the more one earns, the more one pays.

Other important services, whilst not in the same bracket as those mentioned in the previous paragraph, such as water, power, telecommunications, and the like, are supplied to all households at a basic rate. This basic rate, whilst usable, is minimal and if more is wanted then extra is paid for the particular service.

The economy is not controlled by the government, instead the government regulates the laws pertaining to the businesses to ensure that the ordinary people don't lose out in the long run. For example, the government does not tell the factories how much consumer goods must be produced, however it enforces how the workers must be treated, how the environment must be protected, and so on.

Wages are determined by a national wage index, which allocates payments to workers based on duration of work, difficulty of work, and contribution to society as a whole. Difficulty of work is determined by a number of factors, including physical work, workload, and so on. An occupation with a large amount of manual labour will get paid more than an equivalent occupation without the manual labour, for example. The contribution to society as a whole clause is put in to ensure that teachers, doctors and other related professions get paid a slightly higher rate.

This national wage index, like everything else in the country, is amended by votes of the people.
