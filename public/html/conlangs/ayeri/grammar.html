Ayeri Grammar

PRELIMINARY VERSION — NOT FINISHED YET

Introduction

Preface

This is a reference grammar of Ayeri, a South-Western-Kataytanian language. In the following, all rules that are needed to know how this language works shall be discussed. Ayeri might be interesting for speakers of English because it deviates from the common Indo-European set of features in some areas, especially in its verbal morphology.

The name of the language

The language described here is known to speakers of the language itself as well as to outsiders as Ayeri. In Ayeri itself the language is occasionally referred to as tay narano, 'the Language', or nanarano, 'our language'. The word ayeri includes the word aye, 'people', but can theoretically also be broken down to ay|eri, 'by my help'.

Ethnology

The ecosystem of the Ayeri speaking world is largely based on traditional branches like agriculture, herding, fishing, craftsmanship and merchandry. There has not yet been developed an industry as we know it, probably also because of the environmental circumstances which do not allow for an efficient infrastructure. The most often used materials are for example wood, stone, various metals (most notably iron, steel, copper, bronze, gold, silver), clay and bast fibres (as an alternative to wool). <!-- COSMOLOGY? -->

Demography

Ayeri-speaking countries

Click on the image above to see a map of the countries where Ayeri is spoken.

Ayeri is today spoken by about 3.3 million speakers, of which there are about 2,000,000 in the United Western Kingdoms (Lagasimye Akadisuye Nurodanye, LAN), about 800,000 in the Ayeri Democratic Republic (Similitay Ayeri, SAY) and approximately a further 500,000 in the Counciliary Republic of Tuvrang (Dahapasimilitay Tuvrang, DST; all figures according to the 975 KT (= 1999/2000 AD) census).. Since there is not much travel abroad except for trade relations and import/export businesses, most people remain monoglots. Only at the borders of the former Imperium polyglots can be found among common people.

Dialects

Old Ayeri was spoken before this, starting around ... KT, when the Ayeri acquired an own sense of national identity for the first time.

However, it is not completely correct to say that all these 3.3 million people speak literary Ayeri as their first language. Rather it is the local dialect of Ayeri that is spoken in all everyday businesses. All three nations in which Ayeri is spoken occupy an area of about 3,000,000 km² (1,160,000 sq. mi), most parts of the tropical south being covered by a vast, sparsely inhabited rainforest. This isolation has lead to a great diversity in some dialects so that a speaker from the south of the DST has difficulties understanding a person from the very north of the LAN, although both of course claim to speak Ayeri.

The sociolinguistic situation

Multilingualism and language attitudes

As already said, most people are monolingual and only multilingual in the sense of understanding more than their own dialect of the language.

Contexts of use and language choice

In this grammar we will focus on the common Standard Dialect (Ayeri Vihay) that is used in administration and literature. Other than that, the local variety of Ayeri is used for any business.

Viability

With about 3.3 million speakers Ayeri is far from being an endangered language. As Ayeri is omnipresent, ordinary people are usually not pressured to learn a foreign language.

Loan words

Ayeri usually does not obviously contain many words from other language as foreign terms usually get calqued. Possible loan words are mehir, 'tree' and nimpur, 'wine' and pakur, 'disease', as words in -ur are exceedingly rare otherwise

Syllable structure

Syllables can begin with any sound of the consonant inventory except /ŋ/, or they can begin with a vowel, which is very rare. Words can end in any vowel, diphthong or one of the sounds /n m ŋ s r l/. Words ending in /r/ are rare. The most common consonant clusters are those involving two coronals where the first consonant has the highest sonority, or also homorganic stops, or any consonant plus /j/. This latter type of combination can also appear in the onset, the others can only appear in the coda. The consonants /r/ and /l/ can also appear in the onset. There can only be two consonants in a cluster. Considering all this, we have the following basic scheme for a syllable and for a typical disyllabic word respectively:

(C except ŋ (r, l, j)) V (V) C (C except ŋ (r, l, j)) V (V) ((coronal + coronal), mp, mb, ŋg, ŋk) V (V) (n, m, ŋ, r, l, s)

Other consonant combinations are often assimilated, or there may be metathesis when for example /pm/ should occur. /v/ is likely to turn into a /w/ glide after plosives. For more information see this ongoing survey of the syllable structure of Ayeri's stems.

Transcription into Latin

Ayeri is usually written in in a script called Tahano (Nu)hikamu, the "Round Script". An explanation of this and other scripts that have been used can be found on the "Scripts" page of the Tay Benung project (http://benung.freehostia.com/index.php?go=scripts). As mentioned, the standard Latin transcription uses almost the same symbols as the IPA:

/ŋ/ is a special case in that the velar homorganic nasal /ŋg/ is written ng as well in most works. In compounds however, ngg appears when the second part begins with /g/. Likewise /ŋk/, which is usually written nk, except when the second part of the compound already starts with /k/, which makes the combination being written ngk. Some authors use ngg and ngk in general. The macron is usually used to mark vowel length, but it is not unusual to use an acute accent or doubled vowels.

Prosody

Stress

As a rule of thumb, monosyllabic words are usually unstressed, disyllabic words mostly have trochaic stress (stressed-unstressed) and trisyllabic words are iambic (unstressed-stressed) with an unstressed last syllable. In compounds the stress is seperate for the respective parts in that they are stressed as if they were not compounded:

/ˈtrɛn.da/ + /ha.ˈtɑ͡ɪ/ → /ˌtrɛn.da.ha.ˈtɑ͡ɪ/ /ˈma.rin/ + /pʊn.ˈtjɑm/ → /ˌma.ri.pʊn.ˈtjɑm/

Nevertheless, syllables that end in [ŋ] and syllables that contain diphthongs are always considered heavy, so they are always stressed. [ŋ] exceeds diphthongs with regards to syllable weight in this case. The stress of words is shifted accordingly in any case. In some dialects there is a trend towards stem-based stress instead of the usual loose stress. Stress and vowel length usually go together, so that long vowels are heavy as well by default.

Intonation

Simple statements

Statements always have a falling intonation.

Questions

Questions are expressed by a rising intonation.

Orders

Orders are like statements regarding their intonation, except shorter and more intense.

Sarcasm and irony

Sarcasm or irony are expressed by keeping a higher pitch than usual, whether that be statements, questions or orders.

Morphological typology

Traditional morphological typology

Ayeri is an agglutinative language for the greatest part. Typically, there are two morphemes per word, although verbs may have more, resulting in a synthesis index of about 1.4 (The corpus for this survey consisted of a smallish 207 words only, which comprised of 281 morphemes.).

Morphological processes

As already mentioned, Ayeri is a very much agglutinative language, which in this case means that all morphological processes are shown by affixes that usually do not undergo modifications. Ayeri primarily makes use of suffixes as well as suffix stacking, although some things are also shown by prefixes and reduplication, as will be illustrated in the following.

Thanks to the interviews of Bouwer et. al. we finally know that the scheme used for reduplication is the following:

  • {first syllable of verb stem} + {verb stem} + {hardened first phoneme} + a.
  • In the 'reinforcing' morpheme, that is the last morpheme in the compound in this case, bilabials default to [b], alveolars default to [t], palatals and velars default to [k].
  • If the first phoneme is a vowel, the 'stop' is a glottal stop in most dialects, written h, also in the native writing system so as to prevent vowel liaison (e.g. a + a → ā).
Reduplication with nouns

The diminutive expresses a emotional connection to someone or something, or can be used to give a cute or neat shade of meaning to something.

Head/dependent marking

Ayeri is a mixed marking language though heavily leaning towards the dependant-marking side. Verbs for example carry information on the tense and mood of an action, but they also agree with the agent or patient noun phrase (the latter only in intransitive sentences) in gender and number, and they may as well compound with the whole agent noun phrase when its head is a pronoun. Noun phrases are dependant marking for in that the focus morpheme is null for the noun but instead this morpheme is prefixed to the verb. On the other hand, nouns themselves carry infromation about their respective grammatical relation — that is, they are always marked for their respective case. Adjectives are often not marked for anything nor is adjective-related information marked on the adjective. Note that in more dated language, agreement for gender and case of the head exists to a limited amount. Adverbs are not marked at all. Relative pronouns usually just follow their heads like adjectives and other determiners (that is, possessive pronouns), but in ambiguous situations, they may be made to agree with their head in case. Grammatical categories

Nouns

'Stative concepts', as explained in Thomas Payne's Describing Morphosyntax (OUP 1997), are usually expressed as nouns, that is, Ayeri does make a distinction between several parts of speech, of which one is the noun. Nouns do not have other morphological properties than gender (masculine, feminine and neuter animate versus inanimate), which is an inherent property, as well as a plural marker (6.3.), case markers (6.4.), and an indefinite article (6.5.). Further properties that apply to noun phrases, such as element order, will be dealt with later, under 5.3.

Names

Names are different from ordinary nouns in that they cannot receive a suffixed case marker like other nouns. Names also cannot usually take the plural marker -ye. However it would be theoretically possible to say for example "Mandanye", just like you can say "one Peter, two Peters, three Peters" etc. — although of course you hardly ever do that in English, and it is not any different in Ayeri.

  • Number
  • Animacy
  • Gender

Position

Verbs indicating position like "to stand" (beng-), "to sit" (nedra-), "to lie" (hema-) have the same properties as verbs of motion in that the subject of the intransitive sentence is not treated differently from the agent of a transitive sentence.

Time

Adverbs of time are used to specify when an event has taken place. The constituent order for them is not fixed, so adverbs of time may appear at the beginning of a sentence, or at the end, or right after the verb. Adverbs of time include davano (today), tavala (long ago), tamala (yesterday, recently), takala (a moment ago), tasela (tomorrow, soon), tanila (opposite of 'tavala'), tapala (in a moment) as well as edauyi (now), adauyi (then), viaril/tadyaril (sometime(s)), tadoy (never), tadayen (everytime, always).

Degree and measure

Adverbs of degree and measure convey information about either the amount of a thing or the degree of an action. Words from this class of adverbs can be used as regular adjectives as well, but this is a matter of style. Usually, though, they are suffixed to their heads. Adverbs of degree and measure are for example -ikan (many, much, very), -nama (just, only), or -eng (rather).

Direction and location

Adverbs of direction and location answer the question 'where?'. They are for example edaya (here), adaya (there), yāril (somewhere), yanoy (nowhere), yanen (everywhere). The adverb manga indicates motion in general, usually in connection with prepositions. In connection with the verbs saha- (motion towards the speaker) and sara- (motion away from the speaker) one thus gets mangasaha (to here, hither) and mangasara (to there, thither).

Evidential and epistemic

Evidential and epistemic adverbs or phrases that act like adverbs indicate the certainty of the speaker towards something, among these are for example mima (possibly), nilay (probably), nilyang (I think, I guess), paronyang (I believe), surpareng (it seems), tono (certainly, surely) and yoming (maybe, perhaps). Where languages like English would use pragmatic fronting to focus on phrases, Ayeri uses its focus markers on the verb instead of fronting the whole phrase. Transitive sentences almost always have a focused argument, but depending on the intention of the speaker this can be either new or old information.

As Ayeri does not directly follow the notion of Indoeuropean languages to be either SVO or SOV in terms of S either being the nominative or the absolutive, Greenberg's universals cannot be necessarily applied to it, since the notion of subjecthood has been replaced by a general focussing function which may include oblique objects such as benefactives or genitives as well, so strictly speaking it is not necessarily either the agent/nominative argument or the patient/accusative argument that forms the subject.

Noun phrase

Noun phrases consist of the noun proper or a pronoun as their head and one or more adjectives. Adjectives consistantly follow their heads, as described in section 4.3.1.

Question particles and words

Question words often appear at the beginning of the clause, but they may generally appear in the same position as the queried element. Note that Ayeri does not a question particle (see section 2.2.2.2). For a more thorough discussion on the matter of question words see section X.X.X.

  • Sinya queries for a person or thing, like English who, what, and which. It is the counterpart of the agent and patient case.
  • Simin queries for the way something is done, like English how.
  • Siyan queries for a place, like English where. It is the counterpart of the locative case.
  • Sikay queries for the tool used or the circumstance under which something was done. It is the counterpart of the instrumental case.
  • Sikan queries for the amount of something, like English how much and how many.
  • Sitaday queries for the time at which an action takes place, like English when.
  • Sinyisa queries for the reason or cause of an action, like English why and who/what for. It is the counterpart of the causative case.
  • Sina queries for the possessor of something, like English whose. It is the counterpart of the genitive case.

Noun and noun-phrase operations

Compounding

Ayeri's noun compounds are commonly right-branching, that is, the main component comes first, further modifiers follow:

  • Noun + noun: binampadang, 'memory' ← binan, 'picture, painting' + padang, 'mind' (lit. 'mind picture')
  • Noun + verb: deventahān, 'writing system, alphabet' ← deveng, 'system' + tahan-, 'to write' + -an, Nominalizer

Noun compounds are the most common type to occur. A general quirk of Ayeri's compounds is that the elements are often fused so that sometimes it is hard to make out the individual parts of the compound, in that metathesis, assimilation, elision (up to a whole syllable!), and haplology may occur at the component boundary. Case

Ayeri is generally very keen on case marking. Due to its structure the main cases are called Agent and Patient instead of the classic Nominative and Accusative or Ergative and Absolutive. The reason for this is that Ayeri uses a mixed alignment, namely a modified Austronesian alignment system (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austronesian_alignment).

In Ayeri, cases are usually marked by a suffix, except for names and preverbal articles. In these two cases the marker is drawn in front of the proper noun or the verb resepctively in a sometimes slightly modified form.

Articles, determiners, demonstratives

Noun phrases are not usually marked with articles such as English 'a' or 'the'. Focussing a noun phrase makes it more definite by tendency.
