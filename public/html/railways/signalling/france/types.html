2. Signal Types and Location

Colorlight signals: Modern SNCF signals are of the colorlight type, with one lamp+lens housing for each light color+position. Other indicators, such as for speed and direction are also increasingly being replaced with projection assemblies and light arrays. The colorlight aspects are derived from the night indications of the 1941 code mechanical signals. 
Color light designations:

R  = red           r = small red          S* = flashing (color S)
Y  = yellow        y = small yellow       S+ = flashing alternately with Sx
G  = green                                Sx = flashing alternately with S+
B  = blue                                 S? = on or dark due to failure
V  = violet
U  = lunar white   u = small lunar
W  = white

N  = number     (projected or light grid)
X  = letter(s)  (projected or light grid)
Mechanical signals: Most mechanical signals have been replaced by colorlight signals, but a few remain in use. A number of mechanical speed (TIV) and direction indicators are still in use, since the application of projected light indicators is fairly recent. 
Mechanical signal and fixed board designations:

blk = black
tan = tan (light brown)
red = red
yel = yellow
grn = green
blu = blue
vio = violet
wht = white
ltb = light blue

S   = any light of color S as above

color1/color2/color3 shape "symbol"

color1 is the color of the symbol or text
       "*" is used for the case of no symbol or text
color2 is the color of the background
       for multiple colors, they are arranged as they are on the board
color3 is the color of the border
       multiple borders are represented by hyphens, outward to the edge
       "opp" is used to indicate a border around checkerboards that is
          the alternate color
       this field is blank if there is no border

shape is the shape of the board; for rectangles the longest dimension
       is indicated by (--) or (|)

symbol is the text on the board (possibly multi-line), or in some
       cases a more detailed drawing of the symbol


   Examples:

      wht/blk/wht-red square "A"

         white letter A on a square black board, with a white inner border
         surrounded by a red outer border

      */ wht red /opp square board
         red wht

         a square checkerboard of four colors, red border where the square
         is white, white border where the square is red
When mechanical signals are shown, it can be assumed that the clear form will be white when the spectacle is rotated away, unless otherwise stated. Similarly, for 1941 code signals the entire light cluster is shown in the standard arrangement and it may be assumed that the clear form consists of the green or lunar light, unless otherwise stated.

2.1 Signal Locations and Identification

2.1.1 Signal Location

SNCF mainline operations are typically left main for double track running, except for the ex-AL which is right main. Signals are located immediately to the left of or above the track they govern. For double track mainlines with both tracks bidirectionally signaled, the signals for the right track are located immediately to its right (unless on overhead gantries). When a signal is located on the wrong side of the track, a special board indicates this. The signal mast contains an arrow pointing to the track governed (even if on the right side of double track).

2.2.4 SNCFBMVU - Block Manuel de Voie Unique

Single track manual block, using deferred stop signals (Disque).

Operation is similar to BMU, except that block boundaries are only at stations with sidings, and two trains are never allowed in the same block. Before a train is cleared to enter a block, permission must granted from the next block station to allow entry

2.2.5 SNCFGA - Guidon d'Arret - Short Term Stop Signal

Used only in conditions that will cause it to be met at restricting speed (marche à vue or marche en manoeuvre), e.g. preceded by S, (S), M, or pancarte GARE.

GA is used for protection of grade crossings and block limits (BM and «Pontoise-Gisors» BAPR) during switching moves, and is at stop for only short periods of time.
